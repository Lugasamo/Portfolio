Amber Tiles

Ecommerce UX case study

Bridging Amber Tiles' 40-Year Retail Legacy with Modern E-commerce

Amber Tiles had built trust through four decades of showroom service, but its online experience needed to support customers without a staff member there to guide them. The central design question was: how might we make a complex, location-dependent purchase feel as clear and supported online as it does in store?

I led UX and UI design within a cross-functional ecommerce team. My work covered the end-to-end customer journey, interaction patterns and reusable interface system, shaped by the practical constraints of local stores, regional pricing and stock availability.

My role and delivery context

The broader Overdose team delivered the Adobe Commerce platform, systems architecture, HarmoniQ ERP integration, analytics, SEO migration and payments. HarmoniQ kept product, order and customer data in sync, while regional storefronts supported Amber’s franchise model. This case study focuses on the UX and UI decisions I contributed within that wider delivery.

Additional project and delivery context is available in Overdose’s Amber overview.

What shaped the design

Before designing screens, we identified three moments most likely to reduce confidence: prices changing by location, product language that did not match how people planned a room, and forms requesting details before explaining their value. These became practical criteria for the work: establish location early, support different ways of shopping and explain value before asking for commitment.

How I approached it

I moved from discovery and journey mapping into flow design, reusable components and responsive UI. Each review returned to the same questions: can customers understand their location and availability, can they find a path that matches their intent, and is the next step clear?

From insight to interface

Four connected decisions show how those principles became part of the experience: regional pricing, navigation for different shopping journeys, a product-focused visual system and forms that explain their value before requesting information.

01

Location-Aware Pricing Transparency

Amber Tiles operates through a franchise network, so pricing and stock availability vary by location. Customers needed to know which store and price region they were browsing before investing time in a product journey.

I designed an early postcode step that connects the customer to their nearest store, then keeps that regional context visible throughout the experience. Local pricing and availability appear from the start, while the shipping address is checked again at checkout. This makes the system easier to understand and reduces unexpected changes late in the purchase journey.

02

Intuitive Navigation for Diverse Shopping Journeys

Amber serves people who shop in very different ways. Homeowners may begin with a room or an idea, while trade customers often arrive knowing the product and quantity they need. The previous navigation did not support both behaviours clearly.

I redesigned the structure around complementary paths: room-led inspiration for customers still exploring, direct product categories for focused shopping, and a distinct Amber Trade route for business services and pricing. Customers can begin with the mental model that feels natural to them without being forced through the same sequence.

03

Product-Focused Visual Design System

Tiles and bathroom products are highly visual purchases, so the interface needed to support comparison without competing with the photography. I developed a restrained visual system using Amber's earth-toned palette, clear type hierarchy, generous spacing and a consistent product grid.

The same rules were translated into responsive components and reusable CMS blocks. This gave customers a more consistent way to scan products and gave the content team a practical framework for publishing new pages without rebuilding each layout from scratch.

04

Value-First Form Architecture

Several journeys asked customers for personal information before making the benefit of continuing clear. I restructured the forms so each flow first explains what the customer will receive—such as trade pricing, account support or a design consultation—then requests only the information needed for that step.

This sequence was applied across trade registration, consultation and quote journeys. It creates clearer expectations, reduces uncertainty at the point of commitment and gives the team a consistent pattern to measure and refine after launch.

The Result

The redesigned site now stands as a seamless digital extension of Amber Tiles' 40-year retail legacy—delivering the same trust and service customers expect in-store, now at scale. Homeowners planning renovations and trade professionals ordering for clients alike can intuitively discover products tailored to their needs, supported by location-aware pricing that eliminates friction and a streamlined checkout that reduces abandonment.

Connecting constraints to decisions

Challenge → Design response

Each response traces back to a customer, operational or platform constraint.

  1. 01
    Challenge

    Pricing and stock changed across a decentralised franchise network.

    →
    Design response

    Ask for postcode early, assign the correct regional storefront, keep the selected store visible and validate the shipping region at checkout.

  2. 02
    Challenge

    Homeowners explored by room and inspiration, while trade customers arrived with specific products and quantities in mind.

    →
    Design response

    Create complementary room-led, product-led and trade pathways so each audience can begin with its own mental model.

  3. 03
    Challenge

    The online journey needed to carry the confidence and service associated with Amber’s showrooms.

    →
    Design response

    Use visual-first product presentation, clear service prompts and value-led forms that explain the benefit before requesting details.

  4. 04
    Challenge

    Products, orders and customer information needed to remain consistent between commerce and operational systems.

    →
    Design response

    Work with the delivery team around the Adobe Commerce and HarmoniQ integration, reflecting regional and data constraints in the interface.

  5. 05
    Challenge

    The internal team needed to publish varied content without breaking design consistency.

    →
    Design response

    Design reusable interface components and CMS blocks for product, service and editorial content.

  6. 06
    Challenge

    Checkout had to support different payment preferences without adding unnecessary friction.

    →
    Design response

    Create a clear checkout experience around Stripe, Link, cards, mobile wallets and Afterpay.

From ambiguity to delivery

Design Process

The process was not strictly linear. Each stage reduced a different kind of risk and informed the next design decision.

  1. 01

    Discovery

    Aligned retail, franchise and ecommerce needs through stakeholder workshops and a current-state UX review.

  2. 02

    Journey framing

    Mapped how homeowners, renovators and trade customers differed in language, confidence, information needs and purchase intent.

  3. 03

    Risk prioritisation

    Focused first on the journeys most likely to undermine trust: regional assignment, navigation, product discovery, product detail and checkout.

  4. 04

    Flow exploration

    Compared alternative structures and progressively increased fidelity as key decisions were reviewed with stakeholders and delivery teams.

  5. 05

    System design

    Turned approved patterns into responsive components and reusable CMS blocks for products, services and editorial content.

  6. 06

    Delivery collaboration

    Worked with development, architecture, data, analytics and SEO specialists to reconcile customer needs with platform and integration constraints.

  7. 07

    Validation plan

    Defined post-launch questions for analytics and usability review, including region-selection errors, product discovery, form completion and checkout abandonment.
